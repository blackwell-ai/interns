The short version

Atlas is one of the most loved skate shops on the Peninsula: 4.8 stars, an official Nike SB and Vans dealer, a 19-year San Mateo institution and art gallery. None of that is legible to a machine. Its Shopify store ships the modern agent-commerce rails, but the layer that decides whether an AI engine recommends the shop to a new customer is close to empty. When we asked AI answer engines for the best Bay Area skate shops, Atlas was not named once. Its own sibling shop, DLX, was.

Six findings, ordered worst first.

  1. AI engines do not recommend Atlas, even when DLX is named. Asked for the best Bay Area skate shops, AI answer engines returned DLX, FTC, SF Skate Club, 510, and Circle-A. Atlas was absent. It is also missing from the "13 best Bay Area skate shops" listicle that those engines cite.
  2. 4,528 products carry nothing an AI engine can quote. No product page renders any structured data: no price, availability, brand, or SKU in machine-readable form. CCS, Tactics, and Labor expose all of it.
  3. An A-grade reputation that no machine can read. 4.8 stars across 115 Birdeye reviews and 135 on Yelp, and no Organization or LocalBusiness schema linking the shop to any of it. The reputation is stranded.
  4. The homepage tells search engines almost nothing. The title is the single word "Atlas," the meta description is empty, there is no h1, and "Atlas" is one of the most contested names on the web.
  5. The agent-commerce layer is provisioned but empty. Shopify gives Atlas a working UCP/MCP endpoint and an llms.txt, but the content is generic boilerplate and prices are not exposed for agents to read.
  6. A deep editorial archive sits unused. Roughly 990 blog posts, including collaborations with Alien Workshop, Lakai, and Antihero, carry no Article schema and are not wired into search.
ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Mode all default to live web retrieval in 2026. Every query that hits live retrieval reads the same empty machine layer and reaches the same competitors. For a shop whose edge is reputation and curation, being unreadable is the most expensive problem on this list.

Why this is the most expensive finding in the deck

Category recommendation is the moment of intent, a shopper deciding where to buy. Atlas being absent there is worth more than any single ranking position, because the AI engine is making the choice on the shopper's behalf and never offers Atlas as an option. The shop's reputation, which is excellent, cannot rescue a recommendation it is never part of. And the gap compounds: each engine that adds live retrieval reads the same listicles and the same empty entity layer, and reaches the same competitors.

The fix. Recommendability is downstream of the next three findings. A machine-readable entity, structured products, and a reputation linked to the entity are what get a shop pulled into these answers. We also seed the specific category sources these engines cite, so "skate shop San Mateo" and "Peninsula skate shop" resolve to Atlas.

Finding 2 of 6 · Critical

4,528 products carry nothing an AI engine can quote

Quotability is the lowest dimension on the scorecard, an 18, and it is the reason finding 1 happens. Product pages render no JSON-LD at all, the format Google uses for rich results and the format AI shopping engines read to recommend and compare products. The data exists in Shopify, real SKUs and barcodes. It is never published where machines look.

// Any Atlas product page, structured data found
JSON-LD Product schema:   none
Offer / price / availability in schema:   none
AggregateRating / Review:   none

// CCS, Tactics, and Labor all publish full Product + Offer schema;
// Tactics adds AggregateRating (star ratings) on every product.

What this costs in an AI shopping query

When a shopper asks an AI engine "where can I buy the cheapest Spitfire Formula Four wheels" or "which Bay Area shop has the new Nike SB Dunk in stock," the engine quotes shops whose price and availability it can read. It cannot quote Atlas, because there is nothing structured to quote, so it quotes the competitors instead. Across 4,528 products, nearly double the DLX catalog we reviewed last week, that is a large amount of inventory that is invisible at the exact moment of purchase intent.

The fix. Emit Product + Offer schema (price, availability, SKU, brand) across the catalog from the data Shopify already holds, plus AggregateRating where reviews exist. One template change covers all 4,528 products.

Finding 3 of 6 · High severity

An A-grade reputation that no machine can read

This is the finding that should be encouraging. Atlas's reputation is its single best asset, and it is genuinely strong. It is also completely disconnected from the machine-readable entity, so no AI engine can attribute it.

Atlas Skateboard Store: 4.8 stars across 115 reviews (Birdeye), 135 reviews on Yelp, an official Nike SB and Vans dealer, described in coverage as "the best skate boutique along the peninsula." Open since 2007 at 209 2nd Ave, San Mateo. (Birdeye, Yelp, Nike SB, Vans store locator)

Now the problem. There is no Organization or LocalBusiness schema on the site, and no sameAs links connecting atlasskateboarding.com to its Yelp, Google, Nike SB, or social profiles. To an AI engine, the excellent 4.8-star "Atlas Skateboard Store" on Yelp and the anonymous Shopify store at atlasskateboarding.com are not obviously the same business. The reputation and the store are two unlinked records.

The consequence, in the engine's own logic

"Atlas" resolves to an atlas, the Titan, Atlas Obscura, and dozens of brands before it resolves to a San Mateo skate shop. With no entity tying the domain to the 4.8-star local business, the engine has no confident basis to recommend it, even though the underlying sentiment is excellent.

The fix. Organization + LocalBusiness schema (name, San Mateo address, hours, phone), sameAs links to Yelp, Google, Nike SB, and socials, and AggregateRating where appropriate. This connects the 4.8 stars to the entity and is what lets the reputation start doing work in AI answers.

Finding 4 of 6 · High severity

The homepage tells search engines almost nothing

The title tag is the line Google weighs most heavily for ranking, and the line AI engines read first to understand a page. Atlas's is one word.

// atlasskateboarding.com, homepage <head>, today
<title>Atlas</title>
<meta name="description" content="">   // empty
<h1> ... </h1>   // none on the page
<meta property="og:image" content="http://atlasskateboarding.com/...">   // insecure scheme

Why it matters

No "skate shop," no "San Mateo," no brands. The empty meta description means Google writes its own, and there is no h1 to anchor the page. Combined with one of the most ambiguous names on the web, the result is that Atlas ranks for "Atlas" and little else. "Skate shop San Mateo," "Antihero decks Bay Area," and "skate shop near me" do not surface it. This is the discoverability tax under findings 1 and 3.

The fix. Descriptive, keyworded title, meta, and h1 templates across the homepage, collections, and products, plus an https share image. Theme-level and mechanical, measured in hours.

Finding 5 of 6 · High severity

The agent-commerce layer is provisioned but empty

This is the dimension where Atlas scores best on infrastructure and worst on content. Shopify auto-provisions a full agent stack, and Atlas has all of it: an llms.txt, an agents.md, a working UCP/MCP endpoint at /.well-known/ucp, and an agentic-discovery sitemap. The rails an AI shopping agent needs to transact are live. What runs on them is generic.

// atlasskateboarding.com agent surface
/.well-known/ucp        live  (Shopify-provisioned, version 2026-04-08)
llms.txt / agents.md    generic  (~4.4 KB default template, zero brand or catalog content)
agent-readable pricing  none  (no Offer schema; prices require running JavaScript)

Why it matters

To an agent reading the llms.txt today, Atlas is an anonymous Shopify store routing to Shop Pay, not "the San Mateo shop with one of the deepest Antihero, Krooked, and independent-brand selections in the Bay." And while the UCP endpoint can technically take a cart to checkout, the agent has no machine-readable prices to reason over before it gets there. The transaction rails work; the catalog and identity that would make an agent choose Atlas are missing.

The fix. Rewrite llms.txt and agents.md as a real content map (brands, categories, the San Mateo store and gallery, what makes the curation distinct) and expose Offer pricing so agents can read and compare before they transact, keeping the working Shopify transaction layer underneath.

Finding 6 of 6 · Medium severity

A deep editorial archive sits unused

Atlas has an asset most shops would envy and few have: roughly 990 posts across three blogs (news, projects, videos), including artist series and collaboration features with Alien Workshop, Lakai, Stussy, and Antihero. It is exactly the kind of original, branded content AI engines reward, and it is doing almost no work.

Sampled blog posts carry no Article schema, are not wired into the SEO of the products they relate to, and are not referenced from the (generic) llms.txt. The archive is a curation signal that machines currently cannot see. (atlasskateboarding.com/blogs, sitemap_blogs_1.xml)

Why it matters

Original editorial is one of the few durable advantages in AI visibility, because it is hard to fake and it establishes the brand as an authority a machine will quote. Atlas already did the hard part, producing 19 years of it. Wiring it in is cheap, and it strengthens discoverability, quotability, and recommendability at once.

The fix. Article schema across the archive, internal links from posts to the products and brands they feature, and the strongest pieces surfaced in the rewritten llms.txt content map.

Traffic and paid acquisition readiness

You also asked for help with traffic, including Meta ads, beyond SEO and GEO. The same fixes above are what make paid traffic pay off, and the Meta measurement plumbing is not connected yet, so spend today would fly partly blind.

Google Ads (conversion tag AW-841187191) and GA4 (G-3PF1CHM497) are live, with Klaviyo and Attentive connected. But no Meta pixel fires on the storefront, and Shopify's customer-events configuration shows the Meta app slot present with its system-user token empty, so neither the browser pixel nor the Conversions API is sending events to Meta. Tactics, by contrast, runs a live Meta pixel. Advantage+ Shopping and dynamic product ads, the formats that make Meta efficient for a 4,500-product catalog, run on a clean product feed, which is the same structured data finding 2 is missing. Fix the catalog once and you feed Google Shopping, AI shopping, and Meta's catalog together.

The work

Two phases. Phase 1 is a 30-day stabilization sprint that makes Atlas machine-readable and connects the reputation to the entity. Phase 2 is ongoing AI visibility work that moves the composite from F toward B over the following months. After Phase 1 we measure against benchmarks set together and decide whether Phase 2 makes sense.

Phase 1 · 30 days

$1,000

Stabilization sprint

Six remediations, each closing a finding above.

  • Title, meta, and h1 templates across homepage, collections, and products (finding 4)
  • Product + Offer structured data across all 4,528 products (finding 2)
  • Organization + LocalBusiness schema with sameAs links connecting the entity to the 4.8-star reputation (finding 3)
  • llms.txt and agents.md rewritten as a content map; agent-readable pricing exposed (finding 5)
  • Article schema and internal linking across the blog archive (finding 6)
  • Meta pixel and Conversions API connected, product catalog feed prepared for Advantage+ (paid readiness)
Phase 1 benchmarks, finalized at kickoff. Composite AI visibility score (target 44 to 60-plus, F to C or D); rich-result and schema-validator coverage across the catalog; presence in AI category answers for "skate shop San Mateo" and "Peninsula skate shop"; Meta event match quality. $1,000 all-in for Phase 1, refunded in full if the benchmarks are not met.

Phase 2 and beyond

Phase 1 makes Atlas readable. Phase 2 makes it recommended, and keeps it there. This is the work that is not a one-time fix: continuous AI behavior monitoring per dimension with drift alerts; an entity establishment campaign so "Atlas" resolves to the shop across the engines; getting Atlas placed in the category sources AI engines cite; seeding authentic third-party reviews through our independent creator panel; and tracking the schema and protocol changes (UCP, agent commerce) that move every quarter. Phase 2 scope and pricing are discussed after Phase 1 benchmarks land.

For Phase 2 design partners we are standing up a vetted panel of independent reviewers and UGC creators, scored for neutrality, that can seed genuine third-party reviews of your product across YouTube, Reddit, and short-form video. Mentions of that kind are the single strongest correlate of AI visibility, well ahead of backlinks, and they are the part of reputation that on-site schema fixes cannot reach. This is the off-site lever that turns a connected reputation into a recommended one.

How we built this audit

Every finding came from running specific tools and queries against public surfaces, in an order designed to separate what the shop publishes from what machines actually do with it. It is all externally reproducible.

Truth table, frozen first

Before querying any engine, we pulled Atlas's own machine surface directly: homepage and product <head> and JSON-LD, robots.txt, llms.txt, agents.md, /.well-known/ucp, and the product, collection, and blog sitemaps. Counts (4,528 products, 106 collections, roughly 990 posts) come from those sitemaps, pulled June 16, 2026.

AI behavior and recommendability

We ran the full six-engine battery (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Claude, Google AI Overview, Microsoft Copilot) in headed browser sessions, two passes each: from the model's memory with web search off, then with live retrieval on. Logged-in engines ran in incognito or temporary chat (ChatGPT Temporary Chat, Perplexity Incognito) so saved history did not bias the result; Copilot ran as a guest. Each engine and pass was captured to a screenshot. Atlas's absence from the live category answers, and the brands named in its place, is the basis of finding 1.

Reputation corpus

We pulled Atlas's third-party reputation from Yelp, Birdeye, the Nike SB and Vans dealer locators, and local coverage, and checked whether any of it is linked to the site's entity. The 4.8-star rating is real; the missing link to it is finding 3.

Competitive set

CCS, Tactics, and Labor were checked the same day on the same signals (title, entity schema, product schema, meta, Meta pixel, AI category presence). Their dimension scores are estimates from those public signals, not full audits, and are labeled as such on the scorecard.

Reproducibility. Every technical finding is verifiable with view-source, the Schema.org validator, and the store's own robots.txt, sitemap.xml, llms.txt, and /products.json. Reputation and recommendability findings are reproducible by running the same public queries. Counts pulled June 16, 2026.
