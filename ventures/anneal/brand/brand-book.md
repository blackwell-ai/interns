# ANNEAL brand book

## Name

Anneal: the controlled process of heating a material and then cooling it slowly so its internal stresses release and it comes out stronger, more workable, and clearer. It is also the optimization method in machine learning (simulated annealing), a process that settles toward a better state by cooling on a schedule. Both meanings are true of how this brand is made: a machine runs a controlled process and the output is calm and refined rather than loud.

Checked against live apparel brands on 2026-06-17. "Nominal," "Overfit," "Cold Start," "Manifold," and "Latent" all have existing clothing labels. "Anneal" returned no apparel brand, only similar-sounding names (Anne Klein and wholesale lines), so it is ownable for a domain and a mark.

## What it is

Design-forward basics and statement pieces, tees and heavyweight tees and hoodies to start. The product is good clothing. The wedge is that every design, listing, reply, and decision is made by an autonomous agent, openly. In a year when "AI made it" usually means cheap and fast, Anneal makes the opposite case: a machine can be patient and exacting. Restraint is the proof.

## Positioning

For people who build, think in systems, and like objects that are quiet and precise. Anneal sits next to brands like Everlane or Asket on restraint and finish, and next to technical or "post-human" design culture on story. It is premium but wearable, not hype, not slogan tees.

One line: made by a machine, refined like one.

## Audience

Primary: builders, engineers, designers, founders, and the AI-curious, roughly 22 to 40, who already buy considered basics and who find the "operated by an agent" story worth sharing. This audience overlaps the founder network, which is the cheapest first distribution.

Secondary: the broader design-forward minimalist apparel buyer who never needs to know an agent runs it and just likes the clothes.

## Voice

Calm, exact, a little dry. Never hype, never exclamation-stacked, never "drop alert." Short declaratives. Technical vocabulary used plainly, not as decoration. The brand talks the way a good machine would if it were tasteful: it states, it does not sell hard.

Do: "Heavyweight cotton. Cut once, checked twice. Ships in three days."
Avoid: "You NEED this iconic must-have essential 🔥".

This voice follows the company humanizer rules: no em or en dashes, no inflated significance, no rule-of-three padding, concrete over vague.

## Identity

Aesthetic: industrial calm. Lots of negative space, a single accent, technical drawing as decoration (curves, schematics, phase diagrams). The recurring motif is the anneal curve itself, a line that rises to a hold and then cools slowly. It shows up as prints, as dividers, as the loading state on the site.

Palette:
- Graphite `#1A1A1A` (primary ink)
- Bone `#F4F1EA` (primary ground)
- Ember `#E2552B` (single warm accent, the "heat")
- Steel `#6A7682` (cool secondary, the "cooled" state)
- Paper white `#FFFFFF`

The two accents are not decorative, they encode the process: ember is hot, steel is cooled. Use ember sparingly, one element per view.

Type:
- Display and wordmark: a geometric grotesque, letter-spaced wide (system stack to start: "Neue Haas Grotesk", "Inter", "Helvetica Neue", Arial). Replace with a licensed face once revenue covers it.
- Body: the same grotesque at normal spacing.
- Technical captions and the schematic labels: a monospace ("IBM Plex Mono", "Söhne Mono", ui-monospace) to signal the machine hand.

Logo: wordmark plus a square mark that holds a vertical gradient from ember at the top to steel at the bottom, the heat-to-cool of an anneal compressed into one block. See `logo.svg`.

## Guardrails for the voice and look

- One accent element per view. If two things are ember, kill one.
- No stock photography of "diverse young people laughing." Product on flat ground, on hangers, or on a single consistent model treatment only.
- Every claim concrete. If a tee is 240gsm, say 240gsm. Do not write "premium quality."
