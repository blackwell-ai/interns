## Notes

### Business Model and Sales Process

- Pure B2B: Boeing, Airbus, Northrop, Lockheed Martin are core customers
  - 90%+ of revenue from large defense and aerospace OEMs
  - Leads come through industry relationships and committee involvement, not inbound marketing
- Sales cycle: 18 months to 3 years end-to-end
  - Three milestones: business case, qualification path, production
  - Formal supplier onboarding requires extensive contractual, financial, and data protection paperwork
- Customer education is the first real step: showing where additive yields a positive business case
- Titanium focus driven by market concentration: 85% of refined shaped titanium globally is in the A350 and 787
  - Commercial aerospace targeted for volume; defense for higher margins

### Value Propositions and Case Studies

Four core value levers demonstrated:

- Cost savings: 737 engine pylon preform
  - Less material, less final machining vs. traditional forging
  - Projected $7M/year savings (5 parts per pylon, 2 pylons per aircraft)
- Schedule compression: Northrop prototype aircraft
  - Traditional forging path: 12+ months; Norse delivered in ~4 months
- Design flexibility: Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) program
  - Faster part delivery gives engineers ~6 extra months for 3-4 more design iterations before freezing lines and loads
- Factory capacity: ASML semiconductor supplier
  - Reduced billet from 220 kg to 80 kg print, eliminating all roughing operations
  - Supplier avoided ~$20M factory expansion to meet a doubled production rate

### Qualification, Inspection, and Key Constraints

- Qualification is the most time-consuming phase (6 months to 2 years)
  - Additive lacks the 50-year flight database that forgings have
  - Must meet MMPDS (Material Properties Handbook) standards: statistically based sampling across varied heat lots and machines
  - Engineers often require full-scale rig tests and 60-80 flight hours before sign-off
- Biggest yield constraint: 100% volumetric inspection of every part before delivery
  - Max allowable void: 0.4 mm for engine pylon applications
  - Part geometry must be designed around inspection access (UT or radiographic)
  - Often requires adding extra material to enable inspection, reducing the additive weight benefit
